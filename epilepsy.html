LIVING WITH EPILEPSY

Understanding Epilepsy

Epilepsy is a serious brain ailment that is a common neurological disorder across the world. It is characterized by abnormal neurological electrical discharges in the brain. Epileptic seizures are among the most widespread positive indications and symptoms of brain disruption, making epilepsy a significant brain condition. Notably, seizures and epilepsy are among the most frequent neurological disorders affecting children and adolescents, with approximately 470,000 children diagnosed in the United States alone.

EEG Scans and Reports

What is an EEG?

An electroencephalogram (EEG) is a non-invasive diagnostic test used to record brain waves in real time. Diagnosing a person’s specific seizure type(s) can greatly help in selecting the appropriate treatment and understanding what kind of epilepsy one has. This allows doctors to understand the typical brain activity so that they can observe for anomalies, like a seizure. Results from an EEG test are used along with the description of the seizure (from a bystander or caregiver who may have witnessed the seizure) and a physical and neurological exam to help a physician identify the seizure type as well as the type of epilepsy or syndrome.

  • For every patient experiencing seizures/epilepsy , it is recommended that EEG (Electroencephalography) and MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) scans be conducted to comprehensively assess their condition and provide an accurate status update. EEG reports of seizures usually indicate abnormal electrical activity in the brain.
  • During a seizure, the EEG may show rhythmic spikes or sharp waves, which indicate excessive neural activation. The pattern and location of these anomalies can aid in diagnosing the kind of seizure and determining the best therapy. EEG reports frequently include data on background brain activity, which can give insight into the patient's general brain function and help in management decisions.

Here's a list of Do's and Dont's:

Do's' Dont's'
  • Do move objects and/or furniture away from the person having a seizure so that they don’t injure themselves.
  • Do NOT try to hold someone down when they’re having a seizure.
  • JDo time the seizure and call for emergency help if the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes, if there are repeated seizures, or if the person is injured or has difficulty breathing.
  • Do NOT (and I mean NEVER) try to put anything in the mouth of someone having a seizure. (Some think you should put an object like a ruler in the mouth to stop the person having a seizure from biting their tongue, but it’s more likely that you’ll get bitten or they’ll bite a piece off the object and choke on it.)
  • Do make the individual comfortable when the seizure stops.
  • Do consider turning the person on their side after the seizure during the period of recovery.
  • Do expect to find that the person who has had the seizure will be confused and tired afterwards.
